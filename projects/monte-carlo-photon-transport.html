Overview

Light propagation in scattering media is a central problem in biomedical optics, imaging, and optical diagnostics. When multiple scattering dominates, analytical solutions become limited and Monte Carlo simulation becomes the reference method.

The goal of this project was to implement a full photon transport simulator, compute physically meaningful observables, and validate the results against analytical expectations and benchmark configurations.

Project Details

Requirements / Constraints

  • Homogeneous scattering slab with configurable optical properties
  • Henyey–Greenstein phase function for anisotropic scattering
  • Separation of ballistic and diffuse photon components
  • Angular and depth-resolved measurements
  • Statistical convergence with at least 50,000 photons per run

Solution / Architecture

Photon packets are launched normally onto the slab and undergo repeated cycles of free propagation, absorption, and scattering until they leave the medium or their weight becomes negligible.

Implementation

  • Exponential step-length sampling
  • Henyey–Greenstein scattering
  • Implicit absorption
  • Russian roulette termination
  • Angular binning and depth-resolved fluence

Key Results

  • Ballistic transmittance matches Beer–Lambert prediction
  • Diffuse reflectance and transmittance align with benchmarks
  • Angular distributions show expected physical behavior
  • Depth fluence follows diffusion theory beyond one transport mean free path
  • Higher refractive index increases photon confinement
