Monte Carlo Simulation of Photon Transport

Implemented and validated a Monte Carlo model to study ballistic and diffusive light propagation in scattering media.
Domain: Signal Processing / Computational Physics / Biomedical Engineering Tools: C, Monte Carlo, Python, LaTeX Year: 2025 Type: Academic Project

Context & Problem

Light propagation in scattering media is a key problem in biomedical optics and optical diagnostics. Analytical solutions are limited when multiple scattering dominates, making Monte-Carlo methods the reference approach for modeling photon transport.

The objective of this project was to implement a full Monte Carlo photon transport simulation, compute physically meaningful observables, and validate the results against analytical expectations and benchmark configurations.

Requirements / Constraints

  • Homogeneous scattering slab with configurable optical properties
  • Henyey–Greenstein phase function for anisotropic scattering
  • Separation of ballistic and diffuse photon components
  • Angular and depth-resolved measurements
  • Statistical convergence using ≥ 50,000 photons per simulation

Solution / Architecture

Photon packets are launched normally onto the slab and undergo repeated cycles of free propagation, absorption, and scattering until they exit the medium or their weight becomes negligible.

Step lengths are sampled from an exponential distribution, scattering angles follow the Henyey–Greenstein phase function, and absorption is handled using the implicit capture method. Boundary crossings are detected to classify photons as reflected, transmitted, ballistic, or diffuse.

Implementation

Core Algorithm
Photon packet model, exponential step sampling, Russian roulette termination.
Scattering & Absorption
Henyey–Greenstein phase function, implicit absorption.
Post-processing
Solid-angle normalization, angular binning, depth-resolved fluence computation.

Results

  • Ballistic transmittance matches the analytical Beer–Lambert prediction.
  • Diffuse reflectance and transmittance values are consistent with Monte Carlo benchmarks.
  • Angular distributions show expected isotropic behavior at small angles and decay at grazing angles.
  • Depth-resolved fluence follows diffusion theory beyond one transport mean free path.
  • Higher refractive index leads to stronger photon confinement inside the slab.

What I learned / Next steps

  • Importance of validating numerical simulations against analytical limits.
  • Trade-off between statistical noise and computational cost.
  • Clear separation between ballistic and diffusive transport regimes.
  • Possible extension to multilayer media or wavelength-dependent properties.
